Thoughts, experiments, and reflections on design

January 15, 2025

My design manifesto

1. I refuse to design for endless growth. I design for enough: for lives where well‑being, relationships, and ecological limits matter more than output, profit, or "scale."

2. I refuse to treat GDP, engagement, or productivity as signs of success. For me, success means people having time, safety, care, and space to do what truly matters to them.

3. I refuse to design systems that speed life up. Degrowth means slowing down. I design for the real pace of human life: rest, boredom, play, time with loved ones, and unplanned moments.

4. I refuse to turn people into resources. People's attention, emotions, cultures, and data are not raw material for extraction. I design so that people can keep control over their own lives and choices.

5. I refuse to overwrite people's own interests with "optimization goals." My role is to support what people already care about—not to push them toward productivity, self-tracking, or constant improvement.

6. I refuse to design tools that demand people become more flexible, efficient, or available just to survive. A humane system adapts to people; people should not have to bend themselves to fit systems.

7. I refuse to hide harms behind green or ethical marketing. Degrowth design means reducing real harm: less energy use, less waste, less burnout, less pressure—not just better branding.

8. I refuse to design from above for "users" I never meet. Degrowth is about deciding together. I work with the people affected, so that designs grow out of their needs, cultures, and limits.

9. I refuse the myth that bigger is always better. I prefer small, local, and specific projects that truly serve their communities over global solutions that flatten differences and extract value.

10. I refuse to treat communities as markets or testbeds. Communities are not growth channels. They are self‑determining groups of people whose knowledge, desires, and boundaries must be respected.

11. I refuse to ignore hidden labor and hidden costs. When a design depends on unpaid, invisible, or distant work—or on environmental damage—I will try to name it, reduce it, or choose another path.

12. I refuse to design systems that quietly narrow people's options. Degrowth design should open space: more ways to say no, more ways to step back, more ways to live with less pressure and more meaning.

13. I refuse to serve clients or institutions that fundamentally disregard people's well‑being or the planet's limits. I will try to shift their direction; if that fails, I will walk away.
