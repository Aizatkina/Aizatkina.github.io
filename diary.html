My design manifesto

1. I choose conscious speed over constant urgency. Depth is more important for me than volume. I would rather work on two projects with full attention and responsibility than touch ten without care. Slowness, when intentional, leads to clarity, quality, and ethical outcomes.

2. I design for real demand. I do not create for fabricated needs in the form of yearly releases. I design when there is a real problem to solve and real value to deliver.

3. I seek real humans behind the screens. I prioritize talking to people, observing them, and understanding their context. I do not rely solely on surveys, metrics, or abstract personas. Real understanding comes from interaction.

4. I am conscious of the resources we consume: time, energy, attention, and material limits. Design does not demand constant engagement or endless optimization. It allows space—for rest, for thinking, for living outside the product.

5. I design for meaning, not comfort. My end goal is not to remove all friction, but to support conscious choice. I design experiences that help people stay aware, intentional, and present.

6. I reject dark patterns and manipulative growth tactics. I will not use tricks, nagging, or pressure to keep people hooked. Degrowth design respects people's autonomy and their right to step away.

7. I refuse to hide harms behind green or ethical marketing. Degrowth design means reducing real harm: less energy use, less waste, less burnout, less pressure—not just better branding.

8. I refuse to turn people into resources. Users are not funnels, data points, or behavioral inventory. Their attention, emotions, cultures, and data are not raw material. I design so people keep control over their choices and boundaries.

9. I refuse to design tools that demand people become more efficient, flexible, or always available just to survive. A humane system bends around human needs, limits, and rhythms. People should never have to reshape themselves to fit a product.

10. I design with hope, not neutrality. I believe design always influences behavior and values. I want my work to quietly push the world in a better direction—even if it helps only one person. Design is never neutral, so I choose to be intentional about its impact.
